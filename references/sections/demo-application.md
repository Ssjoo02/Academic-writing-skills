# Demo And Application Principles

## Section Role

Use demos and applications to show the work's practical value, upper bound, and potential impact
beyond standard tables.

## Downstream Audience Rule

Design demos for the downstream research community. Before proposing a demo, ask:

1. Which downstream researchers or users should care?
2. What setting would make them understand the work's potential?
3. What evidence would make the demo credible rather than decorative?

## Challenging Data

After the method or benchmark works on target data, test more challenging data or settings when the
claim involves generality, realism, robustness, or safety. Challenging data can reveal the upper
bound and practical limits of the approach.

## Upper Bound And Potential

Applications or demos should help reviewers see what the work can do at its best. They can support:

- real-world value,
- surprising capability,
- stress-test behavior,
- qualitative interpretability,
- user-facing impact.

## Interactive Demo

An interactive demo can be useful when the work benefits from hands-on inspection. If used, the
paper should still include static evidence and clear protocol; do not rely on the demo to replace
experiments.

## Must Avoid

- Demo examples that are visually appealing but unrelated to paper claims.
- Cherry-picked demos without stating selection or failure cases.
- Claiming generality from a small demo set.

## Required Output

For Full Draft Workflow, write English LaTeX prose into the corresponding section file. Keep a
compact `Section Plan`, `Paragraph Plan`, and `Evidence And Risk Notes` internally unless the user
asks to see them. Each demo or application claim must state the supported value, source asset or
evidence, and scope boundary. Run reverse outlining and claim-evidence mapping internally before
returning.

## Self-Check

- Does the demo target a downstream research community?
- Does it use challenging data or realistic conditions?
- Does it reveal capability, limitation, or upper bound?
- Is it tied to a claim in the Writing Policy?
